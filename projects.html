February 2020

My group of 3 fabricated a toy camera for our Intro to Making course. We assembled a frame from laser cut and 3D printed pieces and used an Arduino, electronics, and LEDs to replicate a real camera's functions. You could scope the lens in and out, press a button to turn on the camera , and press a button to identify the color in front of the lens and display it on an LED array. Unfortunately, the LED array broke and isn't assembled in the photos above. Turning on and pressing the shutter button have audio and visual feedback via a piezoelectric speaker and a red LED. I was in charge of programming the electronics and assembling the circuits.

Spring 2019

For my undergraduate capstone, my team of 5 developed an autonomous robot that can automatically adhere to and navigate over a window pane, cross over a barrier, and clean a 5' x 6' total area in under 3 minutes. The device uses ducted propellers to adhere to the window, wheels to move, and a sponge-based mechanism for cleaning. Proximity sensors and an IMU help the robot to navigate. I was in charge of assembling the electronic components and circuitry of the robot. I also helped to develop the control, locomotion, adhesion, sensing, and cleaning subsystems.

More details about this project can be found on our capstone website.

May 2017 - September 2018

I worked on a team of six to design and program video games promoting cognitive engagement and physical activity for Ph.D Candidate Cassie Eng's study on the effect of exergames on the cognitive control and school readiness skills of prekindergarten children.

The games I made were modified versions of the behavioral executive function assessments Flanker and Go/No-Go. The exergame based on the Flanker task became the basis of the study. When playing the Flanker exergame, children stepped on the left or right arrows of a physical game mat depending on the direction that the center fish of a row of five faced. Correct inputs rewarded the child and sped up the pace of the game. The game would adapt if the child could not keep up with the game's pace. The exergame based on Go/No-Go also had the child step left or right on the game mat depending on which side of the screen a balloon showed up and what color it was. I designed concepts and graphics, and programmed game mechanics, levels, menus, and more for both games.

To learn more about this study, see this publication.
